"Copy, Sovereign," Kaelus’s synthesized voice crackled back, distorted by the psychic screaming of the environment. "Squad! Void-Wedge! On me! Odd numbers, high guard! Even numbers, suppression sweep! Move!"

The chaotic scatter of black-armored figures on the screen suddenly snapped into a rigid, geometric shape. It was a triangle of pure violence, a wedge of matte-black plasteel and plasma fire driving into the heart of the crystal forest.

"Zhara," Solon said, not looking away from the feed. "The buffers. Can they handle the proximity density of a tight formation?"

Supreme Warmaster Zhara Nyx was hunched over her console, her face bathed in the green glow of the neural readouts. Her fingers moved in a blur, adjusting the recursive algorithms of the Simulacrum in real-time.

"The resonance feedback is spiking," Zhara reported, her voice tight. "When they stand that close, their individual bio-electric fields are merging. The virus is trying to bridge the gap between their minds, using the proximity to triangulate a signal."

She tapped a sequence of keys, flushing the coolant lines in the soldiers' helmets remotely.

"I am increasing the refresh rate of the logic loops," she said, sweat beading on her forehead. "I'm feeding the buffers a paradox stream—Zeno's Dichotomy overlaid with non-Euclidean geometry proofs. It should keep the virus occupied. But their cortisol levels are climbing, Solon. They are feeling the pressure of the scream even if they can't hear the words."

"Hold them together, Zhara," Solon ordered. "Just get them to the Spire."

To his right, Grand Vizier Kaeva Rhun stood with the stillness of a statue, her hands moving gracefully over her own interface. She wasn't watching the battle; she was watching the world around it. Her screens displayed a spectral analysis of the environment—the shifting light of the sky, the vibration of the ground, the way the crystal trees shattered into dust that didn't fall but drifted upward.

"Sovereign," Kaeva said, her voice cool and analytical. "I am routing the feed capture through the Citadel's primary archival core. We are logging everything. Every wavelength, every temporal distortion."

She swiped a hand, isolating a feed from Unit Nine—a soldier named Varek (no relation to the Confederate analyst, a common name in the northern sectors). Varek’s camera was focused on a cluster of the floating polyhedrons.

"Look at the environment," Kaeva instructed, pointing to the background of the feed. "The landscape is reacting to the violence. The trees aren't just breaking; they are... bleeding."

Solon looked. Where the plasma bolts had sheared through the crystalline trunks, a viscous, silver fluid was leaking out. It didn't drip; it flowed toward the soldiers, moving with a sluggish, intentional purpose.

"The environment is the organism," Kaeva whispered. "The constructs are just the white blood cells. We are fighting the immune system of a dead god."

"Then we burn the infection out," Solon said.

The rear guard, led by Lieutenant Jora, kept the flanks clear, their flamethrowers washing the encroaching silver fluid with waves of thermal retribution.

The feed from Kaelus’s helmet tilted up. The base of the massive black Spire was not empty. Standing before it, half-merged with the crystal structure, were figures. They looked human, but elongated, their limbs stretched and twisted, their skin turned to translucent glass. They held weapons that looked like shards of the Spire itself.

"The Ivory Accord," Solon breathed. "The original inhabitants. They didn't die. They were integrated."

"Sovereign, he's still alive!" Kaelus protested, his voice straining against the synthetic filter.

"Close ranks!" Kaelus ordered. "Gap fill! Leave him! Eyes forward!"

The squad surged past the kneeling soldier. Unit Four didn't try to follow. He stayed on his knees, staring up at the violet sky, his hands falling away from his helmet as the glass figures approached him.

"One down," Solon said, his voice void of emotion. "Thirteen remaining. Distance to objective?"

Solon lit another cigarette, the lighter's click sharp in the silence.

"Let it look," he said, staring at the looming black tower on the screen. "I want it to know who killed it."
