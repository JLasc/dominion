The biting winds of Velis Obscura whispered of a name not yet etched in history, a name destined to be: Cael Dravenhart. It carried no coin, no title, no blood-right, yet trembled with the potential of dominion. Cael, a word once meaning "hollow sky" in a dead tongue, was now reborn in flesh. Dravenhart, a forged identity, a myth he would breathe into reality. His rise was beginning. The world did not know him. Not yet.

The world lay in fractal ruin. Empires had collapsed like lungless giants. Satellites orbited decaying husks of cities below. Nations clung to their banners like desperate men to ledges slick with blood. Somewhere, the stars hummed with secrets. Somewhere, gods died alone. But here, in the Mud Quarter—a district wedged like rotting meat between two broken ridges at the edge of the sprawl-city Velis Obscura—Cael’s story began. The sky above was a torn veil of pale orange haze, where the sun died long before it set. Concrete teeth jutted out of the ground where buildings once stood. Steel pipes pumped lukewarm runoff into open sewers. Drones passed overhead, blind and deaf to the cries below.

Cael lived in a sliver of ruin, a former factory barracks now reduced to rust and whisper. Its halls were choked with squatters, scavvers, dust-dreamers, and the broken. His room, if it could be called that, was bounded by bent scaffolding, oil-stained cloth, and prayers stitched into trash. He had no food, no coin, no comrades, no name others respected. But he was not empty. There was something ancient buried in him, something that had stared at the stars long before he had eyes to lift.

He woke to a sound—scraping metal and the crackle of wet static. The power had returned, briefly. A single flicker of flickering life hissed from the wall. A voice rode the distortion like a serpent in fire: “To rise from ash, one must first be set alight…” It faded. Then silence. Somewhere in the slum, a child screamed. Somewhere else, a gun went off. The city turned, indifferent. But the voice stayed. In his skull. Behind his spine. Ancient. Watching. Waiting. He was Cael Dravenhart, and something beneath this world wanted him to climb.

“Who are you?” Cael thought to himself. The thought struck like flint—not in doubt, not in fear, but in defiance. The question echoed inside his mind, rebounding not as a whisper, but as a presence. The flickering conduit on the wall hummed louder for just a moment—no longer mere static. It spoke again, but not with words. The meaning bypassed language, inserting itself like a needle into thought. A sensation: cold iron submerged in deep water, a mirror with no reflection, an eye that blinks once every thousand years. Then syllables formed, not from his tongue, not from any speaker, but from behind the veil of his mind: “I am the witness to your becoming. The shadow of every empire’s grave. I am unremembered prophecy. The last advisor of the first kings. You may call me… Setharion.” There was a pause, as if it considered him worthy enough to hear its name. The light died. The room plunged into half-dark once more. He was left with his breath, the smell of old oil and mold, and that single name carved into his spine like cold fire: Setharion. He felt no fear, only the sense that something vast and forgotten just tilted its gaze… toward him.

Cael jumped out of bed nervously and clutched the wall. He thought he was going crazy. He started to nervously pant, putting his hands to his head and slowly beating his temples to get the voices to stop. His palms slapped flesh—thud, thud, thud. Each impact echoed in the tiny metal box he called a room. The scaffolding rattled. The wall groaned. Dust fell in little gray flurries like the ash of gods long dead. He was losing it. That’s what he told himself. No food. No sleep. Maybe the black mold got into his blood. He panted like a cornered dog, hot breath fogging against cold rust. But beneath his fear, there was a rhythm—a terrible rightness to the presence that spoke. Like something he’d always known was there, waiting to be remembered. His fingertips trembled as they pressed against the wall. Not cold. Not hot. Alive. Beneath the dermal layer of steel, something pulsed—once. A low *thoom*. Barely perceptible. Like the world’s heartbeat, deep underground. Outside, the city screamed its usual dirge—sirens, arguments, some poor bastard shouting about the cost of synthetic rice. But it all felt thin now, like paper. Like theater. And his breath began to slow… not because he found peace, but because he was being watched. From within. From beneath. From beyond memory. And then, a second pulse came—closer, louder. Not in the wall. Not in the world. Inside his chest. *Thoom*. Like something ancient had begun to knock from behind the door of his bones.

Cael grabbed his chest and hunched over, a single hand pressed against the wall for support. The pain wasn’t sharp, but deep. A pressing, coiling throb that seemed to move with his heart. He slumped against the wall, joints creaking like the scaffolding around him. His hand dragged a smear through oil-dust as he lowered himself, chest tight, eyes twitching. He dug through the nearby clutter—old boots, a shattered terminal screen, a tangle of copper wire and torn denim—until his fingers closed around a cracked plastic bottle, limp from heat warping. Barely a mouthful sloshed inside. He tilted it to his lips—the water was warm, metallic, and carried a faint moldy sting. But it was water. His throat drank it like it was the blood of saints. The shaking didn’t stop entirely, but it was muted now. The moment stretched. He sat there, hunched among his meager belongings, lit only by the faint cityglow leaking through a jagged vent above. The tension faded from agony into something else. Not peace. Not yet. But a calm before. The wall breathed no longer. Setharion was silent. But the pulse in his chest still echoed—fainter now, but there. And the silence around him felt different. Like a trial passed. In the corridor outside, boots crunched glass. Two voices spoke low—harsh. Coded. Armed. Not here for him. Not yet.

Cael fell back onto his makeshift bed and breathed heavily. “It’s over for now. What the hell was that?” he thought to himself. His eyes grew heavy, and he closed them for a moment before drifting back into a slumber. There was no peace in the dreams. His slumber carried him into a place without sky. A void of fractured earth and glimmering glass plains. The stars above pulsed with unreadable glyphs, rearranging themselves as if writing his name—but wrong, over and over, misspelled by alien tongues. He walked there, barefoot. Behind him, the sound of chains dragged across salt. Before him—an obsidian throne suspended in a tower with no walls. It bled light. And sitting atop it was not Setharion, but a figure wrapped in cloth and wire, face hidden beneath a hundred keys. It raised a single finger and spoke in his own voice: “To break the world, you must first survive it.” Then the tower fell, endlessly. And he fell with it. He awakened hours later—eyes dry, back aching. The city sounds were sharper now. Less muffled. It was day. Or what passed for day here. Orange light seeped through the metal slits in the wall like the last rays of a dying furnace. His mouth was dry. The water bottle was empty. His body ached… but something in him felt different. The pulse was gone. But the memory remained. The name Setharion burned faintly beneath his ribs like a brand, long cooled… but never gone. He was still in the slums. Still no coin, no food, no nothing. But he did not rise as the same man who fell asleep. Cael Dravenhart… had been noticed.

Cael got up, filled with partial energy. He fiddled around the room before he remembered that he had somewhere to be. Work most likely, but he couldn't remember if today was his day off or not. He walked to a section of his room where a small cracked mirror hung on the wall. He looked into it and studied his face. The mirror hung by a thread of exposed wiring, swaying slightly with each vibration from the megatrams that rumbled far beneath the slum floor. Its surface was fractured—not shattered, but spidered through with time. Light filtered in through a slit above, catching his reflection in angles. He saw himself in slices. One eye always seemed to look away from the other. One side of his mouth seemed older than the rest of his face. The man staring back had known suffering, but not surrender. His skin bore the gray-tan hue of the Dustlands natives. Lips dry. A faint scar curled along the underside of his chin—of forgotten origin. There was grime along his jawline, and a faint reddish gleam in his eyes that wasn’t there before. The dreams still echoed. The voice. The throne. The keys. But his reflection did not flinch. It watched him, steady. A faint sound crackled through the vent behind him—some broadcast from the city’s outer band repeating in a loop, too garbled to make out. And beneath that: the quiet buzz of drones above the smog ceiling, always hunting, always watching. He couldn't remember the last time he saw his own face clearly. Today, it felt like meeting a stranger. Or perhaps… the beginning of someone else.

Cael stepped back from the mirror, a breath hanging in the air like a question left unanswered. The room felt a touch colder now—not the kind that bites, but the kind that wakes. He reached toward the scattered belongings—what pieces remained useful, functional. His movements were automatic: a faded utility jacket, stiff at the shoulders; worn boots with split seams at the toes, still lined with the dust of old tunnels; a satchel with a strap that creaked when he slung it over his back. He checked inside—something clinked faintly. Familiar weight. Tools, maybe. Papers. Hard to recall until the light hit them right. He secured the cloth divider behind him, more ritual than security, and pushed aside the bent steel frame that marked his room’s boundary. The corridor beyond greeted him with the reek of rust, sweat, and boiling gutter-oil. Sounds drifted from deeper within the hab-stack: someone arguing in a language he didn’t understand, coughing fits, a synth radio spitting old war hymns through bad speakers. He felt it again—the faint residue of the dream. Setharion’s name curled at the edges of his thoughts, but no voice came. Just a faint, electric awareness. Watching. Waiting. Silent… for now. The stairwell loomed ahead, descending into the greater artery of the Mud Quarter. The city breathed below like a wounded beast. It was time to find out what the day demanded of him.

Cael started heading in a familiar direction. He remembered now that he had to make an appearance at work and complete a shift in order to be fed for the day. He lurched forward. As he paced, he started to notice his surroundings and the predicament he and everyone else were in. The stairwell groaned as he descended, each footfall causing rust to whisper from the iron mesh beneath. The walls were covered in graffiti—some old and flaked like dead skin, others fresh with wet paint still dripping. A crude symbol of a burning crown repeated along the descent, stenciled over older marks—someone new was making noise. The deeper he went, the more the air thickened. Diesel and rot. Mold and boiling synthetic broth. He passed makeshift shrines stuffed into wall-niches—flickering holo-icons, charred photos of missing relatives, offerings of meat substitute wrapped in foil. One shrine held a human molar pierced with copper wire. The Mud Quarter opened before him like a wound. Buildings leaned into each other as if exhausted. Scavengers and locals pushed carts of reclaimed tech through puddles slick with nanodust. A rail-thin child sifted through a mound of tangled wires while a nearby adult kept watch with a broken bottle. Above them, an enormous screen played a state-issued propaganda reel on loop: faceless soldiers distributing ration tokens, grinning citizens saluting, the words DUTY BECOMES STRENGTH cycling beneath. He passed a man slumped against a barrel fire, talking to himself in three languages. A little further, a mech-laborer with one arm missing lifted crates with the other, hydraulic fluid trailing behind him like blood. The line between life and survival was gone here. All that was left was the inertia of desperation. He continued walking. Somewhere in the middle distance, a set of vertical floodlights marked the outer fence of the Labor Hub—a gated processing complex wrapped in barbed energy netting and reinforced concrete. A place where men like him traded flesh and time for access. He felt the dull tug in his stomach. Not hunger. Obligation. Today, he must show his face or be cut off. The System didn't call it punishment—only "reset to baseline need." Ahead, a line had already formed. And the towers watched.

Cael stepped into line. Groggy still, but willfully awake. His eyes scattered around, observing others in the line, and the towers. He thought back on how it got so bad for him and how it got to this point. He reminisced on his facial features in the room and recalled how he got his scar and drifted-eye. He slowly stepped forward as the line moved. The line murmured like a river of flesh—half-awake, half-dreaming, half-broken. He stepped into place behind a man with a spinal brace made from rebar and zip ties. In front, a woman rocked gently, mumbling something rhythmic under her breath, eyes fixed on the fence like it might blink back. The towers loomed. Four of them, spaced evenly around the complex. Obsidian black, pulse-lit veins running down their lengths. No visible guards—but he knew better. Automated turrets nested in those towers. He’d seen what they did when someone tried to jump the ration schedule. The smear took two days to wash away. He kept his head low, but his eyes scanned—habit. Taught by fear, refined by hunger. He knew the look of desperation when it festered too long. It was in the twitch of the jaw, the way hands closed too slow, the way shoulders didn't settle. Around him, he counted four like that. He shuffled forward. One step. Then another. His mind wandered—back to his reflection. The scar under his chin. That night. He was younger. A rooftop deal. Short job. Easy creds. He didn't know the buyer worked for one of the Bonehounds. Things went wrong. A blade flashed. He turned his head just in time. The scar came cheap. The drift-eye… not so much. Some black-market tech-stitcher saved it. Mostly. It had been pulling slightly to the left ever since. A reminder. He blinked. The memory faded. His feet crunched forward again. The gates hissed with hydraulic breath as the line thinned near the intake node. A half-sphere drone hovered above each person for three seconds. Scanned the face. Confirmed eligibility. Denied or accepted. He’d seen both. He was six bodies away now. Overhead, a loudspeaker crackled: “Sector 9 labor queue moving. All approved applicants to be processed. All denied applicants to remain still and await tribunal reassessment. Do not leave the line.” A small child cried somewhere behind him. No one turned. No one comforted. Time here had only one direction: forward.

Cael patiently waited for his turn at the head of the line. The line advanced. One by one, the bodies ahead passed beneath the hovering drones, each subjected to their unblinking blue gaze. A quick scan—retinal, thermal, cranial depth. An internal crosscheck with the workgrid archives. Then the judgment. He watched as the man two ahead was halted—a quiet beep. His wrist was marked red by a mist-spray. He did not argue. He did not ask. He simply stepped to the side, head bowed. The woman in front of him made it through. Green mist. Door opened. She vanished inside. And now—he stepped forward. The drone descended. Its shell was smooth, black, and almost organic. No words. Just the high-pitched whine of internal computation. A soft blue light washed over his face. He felt the warmth as it read beneath his skin. Then… a click. A pause. The drone stalled for a beat longer than it should. Something inside him tightened. But then: green. Mist. The side gate hissed open with a hydraulic exhale. He’d been accepted. He stepped through the threshold, and the drone rose behind him like a quiet god returning to the sky. Inside, the Labor Hub stank of oil and sterilizer. Flickering holopanels barked out assignments—Sector Codes, Shift Lengths, Clearance Color. Above it all, a sign read: “SUSTAIN TO SERVE. SERVE TO SUSTAIN.” He was logged. He was expected. And deep within the machinery of this day… he was useful.

Cael checked the holopanel to see what his assignment for the day was. The holopanel flickered—its colors bleeding at the edges like old neon signs drowned in fog. Dozens of names scrolled past, categorized under SECTOR headers and assignment blocks. The formatting was jagged, the touchpad interface beneath it long since de-calibrated, but the display still functioned—barely. His eyes traced the rows. Last name: Dravenhart. Identification: 117-85-KM. Assignment: SECTOR 3D // REFUSE STRIP. Clearance Level: GREY. Duration: 9.5 hours. Hazard Rating: Moderate. Nutrient Credit: 1.6. His stomach twisted slightly at the designation. Sector 3D was on the exterior rim of the Mud Quarter's filtration trench. The “Refuse Strip” was a narrow channel between two processing megatubes—an overflow zone for unsorted tech waste, gutted biomatter, and the occasional corpses no one paid to recover. He’d worked it once before. Not many repeat assignments made it back above without tremors or blood-flecked coughs. But it paid slightly higher—because it had to. He felt the eyes of a drone drift overhead, logging his read, verifying intent to proceed. He heard someone behind him mutter, “3D… damned ghost-shift.” But no one said more. Everyone had their own sentence to serve. A side panel emitted a dull chime, and a compartment hissed open—his loadout capsule. A stained jumpsuit marked WORKERS OF DESIGN across the chest in fading ink. A half-charged filter mask. Steel-toed boots. One rubberized glove. No weapon. A flashing arrow on the far wall blinked yellow. It led down. The refuse waited.

Cael turned around to look at the person who had said something. The voice belonged to a man with a rebreather stitched into his throat, probably former trench crew. His eyes flicked to Cael's as he turned, catching the tone more than the words. He didn’t respond—just offered a faint, pitying smirk through cracked lips, like someone watching another gambler walk into the same losing hand. "Just my luck," Cael replied back. He grabbed his daily equipment. The jumpsuit smelled of synthetic soap and something sour that the wash cycle couldn’t burn out. The boots were heavy, damp at the heel. The glove stuck slightly as he slid it over his hand—inner lining flaking from age. No one talked. Everyone else suited up with the grim efficiency of men and women condemned by rhythm. He headed toward the arrows to a place where he could start to put the gear on. First the jumpsuit, then the boots, and finally the glove. He continued following the yellow markers, down a sloping corridor lit by flickering overhead panels, past a checkpoint manned by an automated torso on a hydraulic track. It scanned him, emitted a bored chirp, and rolled aside to let him through. The hall narrowed. Moisture condensed on the ceiling and dripped down his collar. He reached a final blast door with the words: SECTOR 3D: HUMAN WASTE INDEX - FLUCTUATING. CLEARANCE VERIFIED: CAEL DRAVENHART. LABOR ENGAGED. With a groan like dying metal, the door parted sideways, revealing the entry ramp into the Refuse Strip. It was darker here. The light didn’t pierce through the upper haze. The world was wet with rot and power hum. Vats boiled. Pipes hissed. A conveyor groaned nearby, dragging a bundle of cables wrapped around what may have once been a man’s torso. There were others, scattered along the trench, hunched in faded suits like ghosts—sorting, scraping, stacking, salvaging. He descended. The smell hit him: chemical rot mixed with scorched polymer. A shout echoed somewhere through the gloom. A warning? A joke? No way to tell. Then a chime in his ear. A voice, synthetic, clipped: “Shift start. Unit 117-85-KM. Assigned zone: Trench D-6. Retrieve, sort, isolate high-metal-density items. Biohazard proximity: probable. Compliance required. Completion minimum: 82%.” No greeting. No incentive. Just the rhythm of control. He was in. And the day began.

Cael walked over to Trench D-6 and began working. He picked up junk he had identified as high density, and carried it over to a cart specially made for this debris. The work was grueling. His boots squelched through layers of muck that shifted beneath him like the skin of something still breathing. Trench D-6 was partially submerged—slick metal, broken ribs of old conduit, and heaps of blackened synthflesh twitching with residual heat. He knelt and reached for a chunk of fused plating. It resisted at first—embedded in old cartilage and foamed bioplast—but he pried it free. His glove hissed as it scraped across jagged composite. No blood, not yet, but the friction burned. He hauled the chunk toward the cart—an open-mouthed crate welded onto a rusted dolly with cracked wheels. It tipped under the weight, then settled, creaking in complaint. One of the wheels didn’t rotate. He kicked it. It shifted, slightly. Then again. He returned to the pile. Metal. Wire. Bone. What was high-density was hard to tell without the sensors he wasn’t issued. He worked by instinct. Weight. Sound. The old feel. His back began to scream after an hour. His left knee locked for a moment after kneeling too fast. The mask choked his breath, and inside it, his sweat made everything taste like copper. No one talked. Not the other workers in nearby trenches, silhouetted against clouds of steam. Not the automated collection drone that floated overhead, recording metrics. Not even the voice in his head. Setharion was silent. It was just him. And the filth. And the rhythm of lifting, sorting, moving. The city didn’t see this. The towers didn’t know this pain. But someone benefited from it. Somewhere, someone was richer because of his hands. His cart was nearly half-full now. From somewhere deeper in the trench, a sound: Not machinery. Not human. A soft wet dragging. And a click-click-click, too rhythmic to be random. Too close.

Cael paused mid-lift, pulse tightening. The weight in his arms sagged as his muscles tensed—not in strain, but alertness. He lowered the debris chunk softly—softer than before. The familiar clunk of scrap was swallowed by the trench’s echo, and he crouched low, turning his head toward the sound. That click-click-click continued. Measured. Mechanical. Deliberate. He slid aside a cracked slab of molded rebar plating. Beneath it: wiring, matted polyhair, black sludge. He sifted with one gloved hand, clearing layers with practiced haste until—shift. Something moved beneath. Not away—toward. He recoiled just enough to keep balance. His eyes darted to a pile adjacent—a half-collapsed tangle of conduit and synthetic flesh-bags. The dragging noise was louder now, like claws scraping pipe, wet cloth pulling across the floor. Then he saw it. Between two broken slabs, barely visible through the fog and waste—a glint. Glass? No—an eye. Round. Pale. Artificial. It twitched once. Fixed on him. A shape unfolded slowly. Lanky. Exposed servo-joints. Partially skinned synthetic limbs grafted with actual muscle fibers. No vocal module. Just breath—labored and wet—and the same click-click-click from one malformed hand: three fingers of metal, one too many joints, all ending in blunt-cut glass. It did not leap. It did not speak. It simply rose. Watching. And then, he heard it—inside, not out. A voice. Faint. Static-wrapped. Like a dream bleeding back into wakefulness: “Do not run. You are seen.”

Cael was paralyzed by fear. He breathed heavily. He repeated the words he had just heard back to himself silently: “Don’t run.” His chest heaved. Each breath caught like fabric snagged on a nail. He did not move. He dared not move. The thing watched. Not with hunger. Not with rage. But with recognition. Like a forgotten tool pulled from the depths, he was now of interest. The stench thickened—acrid, antiseptic, rot. The thing’s legs flexed subtly, servo-motors whining beneath layers of stitched sinew and rusted alloy. A low vibration hummed from its chest. He realized it wasn’t a core, not a generator… It was chanting. A rhythm, too mechanical to be human, too raw to be machine. Binary intonation overlaying a mantra-like pulse. Something old. The voice in his skull returned. Not a command. Not even a warning. Just… presence. Setharion, slow and patient: “This one is unchained. A relic. A wanderer beyond cull. It does not serve... yet.” The thing tilted its head. A gesture. Not imitation. Not mimicry. Communication. It raised its hand. Palm open. The fingers twitched, and a small object rolled free—clinked into the filth at his feet. Then, without another sound, the creature retracted. Back into the dark. Back into the steam. Gone. Cael remained, frozen. Before him… half-buried in sludge… a fragment. Wedge-shaped. Metallic. Etched with a spiraling symbol he didn’t recognize—except he did. From the dream. From the tower. From the throne of keys.

Cael reached for the wedge-shaped fragment, only to hear the alarm go off back at the entrance to the Trench. It must be lunchtime. He heard security speakers rattle off commands that dissipated in the distance. He snatched the fragment with practiced speed. It was warm in his palm—too warm. Not molten, not painful, but alive. It vibrated faintly, like a purr or a tremor from something buried far below. He shoved it beneath the collar of his jumpsuit, sliding it into the stitched lining near his ribs. It rested snugly against his skin, and for a moment… silence. Then something stirred inside his head—not Setharion this time, not words. A sensation. Geometry. A structure in his mind uncoiling, folding inward, mapping itself. But just for a moment. Then it was gone. He glanced around. To his left: an older woman hunched over a cart, face covered in a rebreather, unmoving. To his right: steam. No visible eyes. Above: drone still hovering—turned away, scanning others. No one saw. Or if they did, they said nothing. The Trench stirred with motion as workers peeled away from their posts and began heading toward the Consumption Node, a low bunker-like structure at the trench’s edge where protein pouches and nutrient paste were dispensed. The smell of neutral sludge already hung in the air. He was still breathing heavily. The fragment against his ribs did not cool.

Cael started walking normally toward the entrance of the Trench, toward the consumption node. He fell into rhythm with the others. No sudden movements. No glances back. Just one more worker in a gray suit, boots soaked, eyes dulled. The sludge underfoot parted reluctantly as he moved, releasing faint hisses from trapped gas. Overhead, the lights buzzed in irregular patterns, casting strobe-like pulses along the trench walls that made every shadow twitch a beat too long. The Consumption Node squatted at the edge of the trench like a bunker buried in its own failure. Its hatch was open, retractable shutters wheezing as more workers stepped inside—silent, orderly, driven by instinct and obligation. Inside, the atmosphere shifted. It was warmer, artificially so. The lights were steady here. The walls were lined with recessed dispensers—rectangular slots that spit out protein bricks and vitamin-laced paste on command. A console to his left chimed. One worker at a time. Name called. Step forward. Swipe your wrist. Ration dispensed. “Dravenhart, Cael. GREY. Confirmed.” He stepped forward. A strip of light scanned his wrist. A panel opened. Inside, a sealed pack. Clear. Beige paste. One strip of fortified chew-layered grain. No water. He took it. Stepped aside. Around him, others sat against the walls. Some ate quickly. Some slowly. A few simply stared. A man in the corner tore open his pouch but never lifted it to his lips. And through it all, the fragment under his suit burned steady. A pulse without sound. A presence without voice. Waiting. Watching. But for now, this was his moment. Lunch. Thirty minutes. And in his possession—an artifact not meant for this place, nor for him. Yet… it chose to be found.

Cael slipped the ration into his suit for saving. He instead decided to head to the bathroom. An empty stall, so he could further examine the fragment. He moved with quiet purpose, ration in hand, eyes forward. The others didn’t notice—or if they did, they didn’t care. Everyone here was trapped in their own gravity well of fatigue and hunger. The side corridor to the hygiene units was dimly lit and stale. The door to the latrine chamber groaned as it slid open. The smell hit first—industrial cleanser barely masking the scent of too many bodies and too little plumbing. Rows of stalls lined the right. A cracked mirror and a barely-functioning sink to the left. No cameras. Just rust and dim light. He walked the line, boots echoing off metal. Third stall from the end: unoccupied. The door stuck halfway but yielded with a grunt. Inside: a bench, a waste pit, and blessed solitude. He shut the door. Locked it. The moment it clicked, the silence grew dense. Like the air had been waiting. He pulled the ration into the folds of his suit and tucked it deep, then reached beneath his jumpsuit—ribs still warm—and extracted the fragment. It rested in his palm like it belonged there. It was heavier than it looked, but perfectly balanced. Wedge-shaped. Five sides. One end etched with the spiral sigil, the other split open like a key turned inward. He turned it. The etched side gleamed faintly—not reflecting light, but emitting some whisper of it. And then… it shifted. The metal on one edge peeled back, not mechanically, but organically—like petals responding to breath. Inside: a sliver of obsidian. Suspended. Floating. Encased in a thin field of humming static. And behind his eyes—“The first seal is opened.” Setharion’s voice. Calm. Absolute. “You were not made for this world. You were made to break it.” His vision fractured. Not hallucination—overlay. He saw himself. Not now. But possible. Standing atop the walls of a city not yet built, stars behind him, a blade not forged in his hand, speaking a name not yet spoken. Then the vision collapsed. He was back in the stall. Sweating. Hands trembling. The fragment folded shut. Quiet. Waiting. Outside, the timer chimed softly. Lunch: ten minutes remained.

Cael flushed the toilet to pretend like he was actually using the stall. Then he put the fragment back into his suit and headed to the sink. He splashed water on his face and reassured himself he was not crazy. He pounded his temples once more and let out an exacerbated and stressful soft cry. He pulled the stall door open with a practiced nudge, letting it swing closed behind him on rusted hinges. He hit the flush control—an old pressure pad that hissed more than it functioned. Still, the sound was enough. Appearances mattered here. Even in the underbelly. He stepped to the sink. The water dribbled out reluctantly—lukewarm, slightly metallic. He splashed it across his face. The sting woke his nerves, but not his thoughts. He looked into the cracked mirror. Again, he saw himself—not in fragments this time, but whole. Yet he barely recognized the man staring back. His hands moved on their own. Thumbs at his temples. A thump. Then another. Then again. “What the f*ck is happening to me…” He whispered it. Maybe not even aloud. The pressure behind his eyes, the heat in his ribs, the weight of that name—Setharion. He tried to push it out, but it wasn’t something inside him. It was something with him. The cry escaped before he could stop it. Not a sob. Not a scream. Just a choked, breathless release of overwhelm. A sound no one in this place would dare acknowledge. A kind of secret grief. The mirror fogged slightly from his breath. His reflection shimmered for a moment—just a flicker. He thought he saw his eye twitch. The drift one. Turning a little… too much. Then it stopped. The water still trickled. The light still flickered. The world waited. Behind him, the corridor was still empty. The chime for end of break sounded once—long, low, indifferent. His shift called.
