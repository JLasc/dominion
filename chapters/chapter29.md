He made not from mastery, but from instinct. Vanth blinked once, cycling a predictive suggestion. Solon ignored it. “No. If I reroute through the secondary link, I can bypass the shared memory node. That’ll clean the stutter.” His fingers moved again. He grinned—just a little. Not cocky. Just… pleased. They’ll have their own systems. Their own designs. But I want to show them something they haven’t seen yet. He tapped a line of code. The drone schematic flickered, rerouting power through a curved feedback loop. Cleaner. Faster. Almost… beautiful. Solon sat back, eyes catching the rain-streaked glass again. Below him, the world blurred. Ahead—Arken. Challenge. Friction. Proof. But for now—he was building the thing that would carry him there. One line at a time.

The transport broke through the stormline like a blade through velvet, descending in a long, slow arc toward the Arken Spire—its jagged profile rising like the skeleton of a dead god against the silver sky. Lightning flashed far off to the south, but here, above the cold plateau, the air was thin and still. Academion Arken loomed. Built into the black peak of Mount Kheral, it spiraled up from stone and steel into sky, latticed with hovering platforms, energy-bridged towers, and monolithic walls scrawled not with banners—but orders. No statues. No ceremony. Just imperatives—etched in steel, translated across every dialect of the Imperium.

“YOU ARE THE FLAME’S SHADOW.”
“YOUR WILL IS YOUR NAME.”
“ADAPT. RESIST. ASCEND.”

The transport docked without sound. The moment the doors hissed open, the atmosphere changed. Gone was the patient air of Havenspire. Here—precision ruled. Brutality followed. A gust of cold air knifed into the pod as the ramp extended. Solon stepped forward with his travel cloak drawn close, Vanth dimmed on his wrist. He took a single step into the open and barely got a second breath— “MOVE!” “OUT OF THE SHUTTLE, CYCLE-SPAWN!” “DO YOU THINK THIS IS A GILDED SOFTHOUSE?!”

Three figures descended on him before his feet were even flat on the tarmac—Arken Wardens, clad in matte black utility armor, masks sealed, voices projected through vocal amplifiers that turned speech into thunder. One of them moved so close Solon could see his own distorted reflection in the visor. “You’ve got five seconds to join your age cohort before I drag you there by the scruff like a wet civ-kit!” “Move, now! MOVE!”

Solon didn’t speak. He moved. He adjusted the strap across his chest, drew his hood back, and joined the line of other First Cycle Inductees—a dozen youths around his age, every one of them looking more weathered, more hardened, more street-wired than he was. Some glanced at him—brief, assessing. One girl with a buzzcut and a scar beneath her eye sneered. Another boy to her right tilted his head at Solon’s bracer and muttered something he couldn’t hear. He didn’t flinch. He didn’t make eye contact. He just filed in, exactly as the Warden demanded.

Another bark followed: “STAND STRAIGHT. NO SPEAKING. HANDS AT YOUR SIDES. YOU’RE NOT PEOPLE YET. YOU’RE RAW STEEL. AND RAW STEEL IS WORTHLESS UNTIL IT’S CUT.” Solon stood still. Rain hit the concrete around them, slick and thin. The wind howled off the tower walls. Arken was nothing like the villa. There were no smiles. No soft words. No reassurance. This was not a place to be protected. This was a place to be forged. And Solon—awkward, brilliant, sharp at the wrong angles—was already feeling the heat. This is what he wanted. Challenge. Friction. Shape. He locked his jaw. The gates of Arken opened. And Solon Dravenhart walked through.
